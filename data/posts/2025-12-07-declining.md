## The better-than-ChatGPT tool to search.

You won’t like this answer, but… Grok.

Grok is so, so goooood to search.

Better than ChatGPT. Better than Perplexity.

I discovered it by asking a question about something I knew.

I asked ChatGPT and Perplexity the following:

“What happened with the Coca-Cola AI ad?”

Neither of them understood the depth of the topic.

![from Perplexity](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/0f8bf7f5-72e7-40fc-a0e3-d1db01c05837_2414x1398.png)
*from Perplexity*

![from ChatGPT](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/e3062fe7-34b4-4b44-b8ee-5cb07872d9a2_2482x906.png)
*from ChatGPT*

But this is terrible journalism. This is not what truly happened.

I asked the same to Grok, and Grok actually knows what happened.

It can search the web properly:

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/90ea0e97-34f3-4aa2-9653-89bee0ccf6b4_2098x1054.png)

ChatGPT and Perplexity stayed at surface levels, raging on “*look, the 90 billion dollar company used AI!!!*”. Grok is thinking a layer deeper, understanding the obvious intention (to ragebait us with semi-good AI).

How come?

It’s because Grok is directly connected to X (=Twitter). And X is the town hall of the internet, where everything happens first.

And Grok does not stop here. The team built an open-source alternative to Wikipedia called **[Grokipedia](https://grokipedia.com/)**. Elon Musk calls Grok himself a “truth seeker”.

It wasn’t good enough 6 months ago.

It now became my go-to search tool.

#### Veo-3.1 for videos.

It’s the (new) video model from Gemini.

I use Genspark to access every video model (without paying for 10+ subscriptions).

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/482d2501-db68-405c-bc10-e15d7a136807_3424x1998.png)

The best feature is called “**First-to-Last Frame**”.

1. You select your first frame of the video.2. You select the last frame of the video.3. Veo imagines what’s between them.

It forces you to think in terms of a storyboard. And that’s the best way to combine control and quality with AI.

#### Claude prompts to write.

My favorite ways to write with Claude:

1. Use “Custom style” and train it on your style.2. Search first, then write. Here’s an example:

> Act as my newsletter research lead for “How to AI” (180K+ subscribers on Substack). Your job: find my next Sunday newsletter topic.What performs well for my audience:Counterintuitive AI findings (e.g., my hit “Rude” — an arxiv paper showing rudeness improves ChatGPT outputs)Major product launches the week they drop (ChatGPT-5, new Claude features, etc.)Practical “hidden feature” deep-divesContrarian takes backed by dataYour research plan:Search this week’s AI news for anything with viral potentialCheck arxiv.org for recent AI/LLM papers with surprising or counterintuitive findingsLook for underreported announcements from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, MetaScan for emerging controversies or debates in AIDeliver 10 topic + angle combinations: For each, give me:Topic (2-5 words)Angle (the specific hook that makes it newsletter-worthy)Source (link)Why it fits (one sentence on why my audience would care)Prioritize “I didn’t know that” moments over obvious news everyone’s already covered. Start by searching now — I need fresh options, not hypotheticals.

#### Grok prompts to search.

Here are examples showcasing its power:

**Real-Time Event Tracking via X**

> Prompt: “Act as a social media intelligence analyst. Your goal is to provide a comprehensive, real-time overview of a breaking news event, defining success as synthesizing 20+ recent X posts with web news sources to highlight key narratives, public sentiment, and emerging trends. Search X for posts from the last 24 hours using semantic and keyword queries like ‘(event name) filter:news min_faves:50 since:YYYY-MM-DD’, then cross-reference with top web search results for verified facts.”

**Sentiment Analysis on Controversial Topics**

> Prompt: “Act as a public opinion researcher. Your goal is to gauge polarized views on a hot-button issue, defining success as categorizing 30+ X posts into pro/con/neutral buckets with engagement metrics, backed by web snippets for context. Employ X semantic search with a query like ‘debate on [topic]’ excluding biased sources, and web search with snippets for ‘site:reddit.com [topic] OR site:news.com [topic]’.”

**Fact-Checking Viral Claims with X Evidence**

> Prompt: “Act as a misinformation detective. Your goal is to debunk or confirm a viral claim, defining success as tracing its origin post, amplification chain (50+ retweets/quotes), and counter-evidence from credible web sources. Use X keyword search for ‘exact phrase of claim retweets_of_user_id:ID min_replies:10’, then web search with snippets for authoritative sites like ‘site:factcheck.org [claim]’.”

**Academic Literature Review**

> Prompt: “Act as a literature reviewer. Your goal is to summarize recent papers on a scientific topic, defining success as categorizing 8 studies by methodology, findings, and gaps, with citation links. Search web_search ‘topic review 2025 filetype:pdf site:arxiv.org OR site:pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov’ num_results:15, then browse_page on abstracts with instructions to ‘pull methods, results, and DOIs’.”

**Legal Precedent Analysis**

> Prompt: “Act as a legal researcher. Your goal is to analyze case law on a statute, defining success as tracing 12 precedents with outcomes, jurisdictions, and evolving interpretations. Use web_search ‘statute name case law site:supremecourt.gov OR site:oyez.org’, chain to browse_page on judgments instructing ‘summarize holdings, dissents, and citations’.”

**Competitive Landscape Mapping**

> Prompt: “Act as a business strategist. Your goal is to map competitors in an industry, defining success as a SWOT matrix for 5 firms from annual reports and news. Employ web_search ‘industry competitors 2025 analysis site:forbes.com OR site:bloomberg.com’ num_results:20, use code_execution to tabulate data, and browse_page on 10-K filings for financials.”
