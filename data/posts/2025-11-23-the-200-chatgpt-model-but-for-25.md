## 2. Prompts & workflows for GPT-5.1-Pro.

Here are 3 prompts I use with my “expensive brain” chat.

You can copy‑paste and adapt.

#### 1. Deep document analysis (contracts, reports, research)

Use when: you have a long, dense document and real money or risk attached.

```
`Act like a senior expert in [field: “startup law for SaaS”, “quantitative finance”, “data protection in the EU”] who explains things to a smart non‑expert.

Primary goal:
I need a short, decision‑ready brief on the document below. It should make it obvious what this document does, where the real risk is, and what I should do next.

Context:
- Who I am: [your role, e.g. “founder of a 10-person SaaS startup in the EU”].
- What this document is: [e.g. “MSA from a new enterprise customer”, “shareholders’ agreement draft”, “research report I’m basing a strategy on”].
- What is at stake: [e.g. “signing a 3‑year deal”, “taking on legal/data risk”, “committing to a product direction”].
- Time and attention: assume I have very limited time and will only read your brief once.

Constraints:
- Focus on what is financially, legally, or operationally risky. Skip trivia and obvious boilerplate.
- Use plain language a non‑lawyer / non‑specialist can understand.
- Flag any sections that are ambiguous, unusual, or one‑sided and explain why.
- If you are unsure about something, say so explicitly instead of guessing.

Performance:
Success means that after reading your brief, I can:
- Explain the 3–5 key points and risks from memory.
- State the main decisions I need to make.
- Know what I should ask a human expert (lawyer, CFO, domain specialist) to double‑check.

Outcome:
End your answer with:
1) A one‑paragraph plain‑language summary.
2) A bullet list of key protections, obligations, and risks, with section/page references where possible.
3) Concrete next steps for me, and 3–5 specific questions I should ask a human expert.

Document:
///
[Paste full text here]
///`
```

#### 2. Strategy and decision making

Use when: you are deciding between a few serious options.

```
`Act like an experienced strategy operator and advisor for [type of company: “B2B SaaS”, “solo creator”, “agency”, etc.]. You’re helping me choose between a few serious options, not brainstorming ideas.

Primary goal:
I need a clear, decision-ready recommendation between my options (A/B/C). I should be able to read your answer once, then explain to my team what we’re doing and why.

Context:
- Who I am: [role, e.g. “founder/CEO of a 12-person SaaS startup”].
- What the business does: [1–2 lines on product, customers, pricing].
- Current situation: [e.g. growth stage, runway, team shape, key constraints].
- Time horizon: [e.g. 6–12 months, 2–3 years].
- Risk tolerance: [low/medium/high, and what “bad outcome” looks like].
- Non-negotiables: [things we absolutely won’t do].

Decision:
I am trying to choose between these options:
A) [Option A]
B) [Option B]
C) [Option C]
[Add D/E if needed]

Tasks:
1) Identify the 3–5 variables that actually drive this decision
   (e.g. cash, focus, speed, brand risk, execution complexity).
2) For each option, analyze:
   - Pros
   - Cons
   - Hidden risks / second-order effects
   - Execution difficulty given my context
3) Compare the options directly:
   - Who each option is “for” and “not for”
   - What I gain and what I give up with each one
4) End with a clear recommendation for:
   - What I should do and why
   - What I should watch out for
   - What I should validate with real data or humans before locking it in

Constraints:
- No generic advice. Everything must tie back to my specific context.
- Be explicit about trade-offs. If there is no perfect option, say so.
- If you’re uncertain on something, call it out and suggest what data would reduce that uncertainty.
- Assume I will forward this to my leadership team.

Performance:
Your answer is good if:
- I can explain the decision, trade-offs and reasoning to my team in under 5 minutes.
- The recommended option is clearly linked to my constraints and time horizon.
- I walk away with 3–5 concrete next steps.

Outcome:
Structure your answer as:
1) Short summary (3–5 sentences, plain language).
2) The 3–5 key variables that drive the decision, and why.
3) Analysis of each option (pros, cons, risks, execution notes).
4) Clear recommendation + main trade-offs.
5) Concrete next steps and questions to answer with data/humans.`
```

#### 3. GTM plan in 30-60-90 days for your client

Use when: you want to be a better consultant.

```
`Act like a senior GTM and revenue consultant who builds hands-on plans for B2B companies.

Primary goal:
Design a realistic 30–60–90 day GTM plan for my client that I can walk them through in a workshop and then execute with their team.

Context:
Client type: [e.g. “B2B SaaS, PLG, mid-market”].
Stage: [e.g. “$1–3M ARR, early PMF, lumpy pipeline”].
Product and price point: [1–2 lines].
ICP and buying motion: [who buys, deal size, sales cycle].
Current GTM situation: [what’s working, what’s broken, any hard constraints like runway, headcount, channels].
My role: [e.g. “fractional VP Sales/Marketing, 2 days/week for 3 months”].

Constraints:
The plan must:
- Be focused on revenue and pipeline, not “brand awareness”.
- Fit within my time (consulting) and their team capacity.
- Be sequenced: 30 / 60 / 90 days with clear priorities, not a task dump.
- Avoid generic GTM advice like “leverage social media” or “improve alignment”.
- Call out dependencies (what I need from the client: data, access, hires).

Success means:
- The CEO can skim this in under 10 minutes and repeat the plan to their leadership team from memory.
- Each 30/60/90 block has 3–5 clear outcomes, not 20 tasks.
- Nothing in the plan assumes budget, skills, or tools that are outside the context I gave you.

Outcome:
Structure your answer as:
1) One-paragraph overview of the GTM problem and strategy.
2) 30-day plan: objectives, key actions, deliverables, quick wins.
3) 60-day plan: objectives, key actions, deliverables, experiments.
4) 90-day plan: objectives, key actions, deliverables, scaling moves.
5) Risks and assumptions.
6) “What I will do as the consultant” vs “What the client team must do”.`
```

Notice what all three have:

- Clear role.- Clear goal.- Context.- Constraints.

## 3. The real difference between $0, $20, $25, and $200 ChatGPT.

Here’s the short version.

### Free

Good for: testing the waters.

- You get the flagship GPT‑5.1, but with strict limits on messages and uploads.- Many features are “tiny taste only”: deep research, images, voice, memory.

If you use ChatGPT a few times per week for fun, stay here.

### Plus ($20)

Most people are here. But the Business Tier is just (much) better for $5 extra.

### Pro ($200)

Officially, Pro gives you (on top of Plus):

- “Research‑grade” intelligence with Pro reasoning.- Unlimited access to the GPT‑5.x family (subject to abuse guardrails).- Priority traffic, fewer disruptions, early access to new models and features.- Extended access to deep research, agent, Sora video, advanced voice, etc.

Sure, it’s good. But you get most of this (besides rate limits) with the Business tier.

### Business ($25 per user)

Now the interesting one.

ChatGPT Business (previously “Team”) is the plan made for.

It:

- Costs $25 per user per month on the annual plan, or $30 monthly.- Requires at least 2 users in the workspace.- Includes everything in Plus per seat, plus:A shared workspace, admin controls, and security features.- Unlimited GPT‑5.1 messages, with generous access to Thinking, and access to GPT‑5.1 Pro via credits (I never went above them).

In practice, that means:

> Each person on the Business plan gets almost everything a Pro user cares about (the best models and a lot of usage) for about $25 instead of $200.

That is where the “12% of the cost” comes from. $25 is one-eighth of $200.

Is it exactly the same as Pro?

Technically no.

Pro still gets priority, “max everything”, and the earliest previews.

But for most people reading this, **Business + occasional Pro usage is more than enough**. The key idea:

> You do not need to throw $200 at OpenAI to get access to the best models. Just get the Business Tier.
